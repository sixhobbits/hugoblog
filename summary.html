Translational Metabolomics: Current Challenges and Future Opportunities Farhana R. Pinu 1,* , Seyed Ali Goldansaz 2,3 and Jacob Jaine 4

Summary
Abstract
  • Metabolomics is a hot area: it’s new, but has proven breakthroughs in science
  • Many breakthroughs haven’t yet made their way into clinics
  • We need to improve the throughput of research -> industry
introduction
  • Initial focus was on untargeted metabolomics - try to understand all metabolites at once
  • This was too ambitious - we now mainly use targeted metabolomics, focus on hundreds at at time
  • From a science perspective, the goal is to Understand how organisms respond to internal and external alterations
  • (Example: find ‘biomarkers’ - micro indications that a diseases is present before onset of symptoms)
  • There’s a spotlight on metabolomics in research settings now, but this will fade if we don’t move the field towards more applied settings
Translational omics
  • We need to “translate” experimental results into turnkey products
  • Traditionally, this was not a concern of academic researchers, but now it is
  • The HGP (Human Genome Porject) has given us vast amounts of data to work with
  • IOM (Institution of Medicine) produces guidelines on how to do effective ‘translation’
  • Most popular genetic tests are: ** Prenatal tests ** Hereditory cancer tests
  • Gaining ground in “personalised medicine” - finding the best treatment for an individual instead of a disease
Challenges in Translational Metabolomics
  • Steps to a successful translation ** Robust experimental design ** Data Acquisition ** Data Mining ** Intperetation ** Validation of candidate biomarkers

Failure in any of the steps prevents translation to clinics or industry.

Perceptions (change title)
  • Metabolomics isn’t as well known in funding bodies and the media
  • Often seen as hyped: ‘over promising and under delivering’
  • So much data that looks promising, but takes time to process it all and reach its full potential
  • Metabolomics community should make successes more public, use social media etc
Costs
  • Costs nearly a million dollars for an LC-MS system. Similar for NMR.
  • Labs need mutliple systems
  • Each sample is costly, so often sample numbers are reduced, leading to worse results
Expertise
  • Need multi-disciplinary experts including biologists, analytical chemists, statisticians, data scientists, and bioinformaticians
  • Larger teams to account for this
Data acquisition
  • Blood and derivatives (serum, plasma) are the most widely explored biofluids for acquiring data
  • Challenging to capture a complete metabolome of a biological entity using a single analysis method
  • Very diverse metabolites - you might need specific extraction methods, or some might be destroyed by e.g. light
  • There is no universal analytical method
  • Metabolites with low concentration need specialised equipment (e.g. mass spectrometery), but this equipment can’t measure those with high concentrations.
  • Need better platforms and data analysis
Metabolite Identification and Pathway Mapping
  • There are two ways to identify metabolites
  • The easy way is to identify features - e.g. known mass and retention time
  • “True” identification is much harder - need an extensive metabolite library
  • We need more platforms and tools to help with “True” identification

Quantification
  • Most of the data we generate is “semi quantitative”
  • We need more “absolute quantification”
  • Requires a calibration curve with different concentrations
  • Allows for shared research and easier translation to real-world products
  • Very difficult: each step has to be exact and quality assurance is paramount
  • The Metabolomics Society has also an oversight committee named “Metabolomics Standards Initiative (MSI)” to monitor and review the standardization of metabolomics workflows
Translational opportunities
  • Glamour topics like Cancer and Diabetes can’t be solved in a reasonable timeframe
  • Research is important, but won’t help us translate to real products
  • Find a smaller or more localised problem and solve it, then publish results
  • After peer review, translate the results into a portable device that can be used in the field
  • We have the tech to allow people to access biomarkers in the palm of their hand

Simplification
  • Trend that a study is a success if someone can successfully differentiate between two groups (e.g. healthy and diseased animals)
  • But this sometimes relies on hundrends of metabolites, and can’t easily be translated to a portable device
  • Researchers need to focus on fewer variables - the fewer the better if we want to use them in commercial settings
Commercialism
  • Needs to be profitable, which means people need to use it
  • Either it must provide enough benefit that people use it voluntarily, or because it is legally enforced
  • Researchers need to involve industry parteners from the start and ground their work in industrial and societal problems
Test format and miniaturisation

  • We need to transform expensive and laborious processes to tools that are ** hand-held and easy to use ** rapid ** low cost ** easy to interpret

  • Academics often consider all of these ‘outside scope’ but they are vital for the translation process

  • Example instance, Sciex has developed a benchtop, affordable and user-friendly In Vitro Diagnostic (IVD) LC-MS/MS instrumentation for clinical diagnostic laboratories (https://sciex.com/diagnostics).

Clinical applications
diagnosis and biomarker discovery
  • Amino acid profiling identifies people at high risk of type 2 diabetes
  • oncometabolites help diagnose cancer
  • Many diseases that were assumed to be genetic now seem to be due to metabolic disorders
Personalised medicine
  • Identify drug targets
  • Finding the connection between metabolites and diseases.
  • e.g. connection between Alzheimers and disturbed glucose and lipid metabolism
Industrial applications
  • Essential tool for bioenergy and fermentation industries.
  • Beer, Wine, Bread, Soy Sauce
  • Bio-energy and gas fementation
Recommendations
  • Absolute quantification needs to be improved
  • More miniaturised instrumentation
  • More automated data processing
  • Better following of guidelines from professional and regulatory bodies
  • Better platforms for multi-omics integration
  • Forums and symposiums to collaborate between professional bodies, govt, regulators, and funding bodies
  • Publish outcomes to journals, newspaper and social media
